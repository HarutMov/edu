The problem isn't the math.
It's the fetching.

Almost everything a neural network does comes down to multiplying matrices. And multiplying matrices comes down to one tiny operation, repeated an absurd number of times:

multiply two numbers, add the answer to a running total. That's the whole operation. Hardware people call it a MAC — multiply-accumulate.

A chip can do that ridiculously fast. The trouble is feeding it. Done naively, every multiply needs two fresh numbers dragged out of memory — and a trip to memory burns hundreds of times more energy than the multiply you're doing with it. You can build the world's fastest multiplier and it will still spend its life waiting for groceries to show up.

So the real question was never "how do we multiply faster?" It was "how do we get more mileage out of each number we bother to fetch?"

The idea: pass it to your neighbour

Picture a warehouse where every worker walks back to the loading dock for every single part. Now picture a bucket brigade instead: one trip to the dock, then the part gets handed down the line — and everyone it passes gets to use it.

That's a systolic array. Fetch a number once, then let it walk across a grid of tiny cells, getting used at every stop it makes.

The name gives it away. Systole is the contraction of a heart. Data here doesn't get fetched, it gets pumped — in a steady rhythm, one beat at a time, with the whole grid twitching in time with it.

One cell, four rules

Each cell is deliberately, almost insultingly dumb. It owns a multiplier, an adder, two registers, and no idea what's going on. Every beat it does exactly four things:

  • Take a number a from the neighbour on its left.
  • Take a number b from the neighbour above.
  • Add a×b to its running total.
  • Pass a along to the right, and b along down.

No memory access. No instructions to decode. No branches. No idea what its neighbours are doing. That poverty is the point — it's why you can etch tens of thousands of them onto one chip and clock them all in lockstep.

Wire them into a grid

Now tile those cells into a 3×3 grid to compute C = A × B. The wiring is the whole trick and it's almost too simple: each cell owns exactly one answer. The cell at row i, column j spends its entire life building C[i][j], and never moves it anywhere.

Which tells you how to feed it. Send row i of A in from the left of row i, and column j of B down from the top of column j. Now every number that walks through a cell is a number that cell actually needs.

Why the input looks like a staircase

You'll see the numbers queued up outside the grid in a skewed staircase. That's not styling — it's the timing, and here's the entire reason for it:

B[0][0] gets used by the top-left cell on beat 0. Then it has to walk to the cell below, which costs a beat. So it lands there on beat 1 — which means row 1 of A has to set off one beat late to meet it.

Every row starts one beat after the row above it. Every column starts one beat after the column to its left. Stack those delays up and you've drawn a staircase.

It's a rendezvous problem. Two numbers have to reach the same cell on the same beat, and they've travelled different distances to get there — so they leave at different times. Nothing in the array ever checks whether its partner turned up. The schedule just guarantees it did.

So what did that buy us?

The grid finished 27 multiply-accumulates in 7 beats, because up to seven cells were working at once. Nice — but the speedup isn't the point. Look at the memory row.

Eighteen reads instead of fifty-four, because each number gets fetched once and then used at every cell it walks past. And that ratio isn't a constant — it's the width of the array. Make the grid n cells wide and every number you fetch gets used n times.

Google's first TPU ran a 256×256 grid of these cells: 65,536 multipliers, the lot of them fed by a bucket brigade. Every number pulled in from memory gets used 256 times before it's retired. Since fetching costs hundreds of times more energy than multiplying, that ratio is the entire win. The speed is a side effect of not being hungry.

The catch

Look at the pulse under the array again — that shape is the honest story. The grid works at 1, 3, 6, 7, 6, 3, 1 cells per beat. It is never once fully busy.

  • Fill and drain. At the start the wavefront hasn't spread yet; at the end it's walking out. Across the whole run only 43% of the array's capacity got used. Real chips hide this by streaming tile after tile back-to-back, so one matrix's drain overlaps the next one's fill and the pulse flattens into a plateau.
  • It does exactly one thing. The rigid lockstep schedule is what makes the cells cheap enough to build by the thousand — and it also means the hardware is dead weight for anything not shaped like a matrix multiply.
  • Your matrix doesn't fit. Real ones dwarf the grid, so they get chopped into tiles, streamed through, and stitched back together.

The version animated here is output-stationary: the answers sit still, both inputs move. It's the clearest one to watch. Google's TPU is weight-stationary — weights are preloaded into the cells and only the activations flow through — which suits running the same weights against a stream of inputs. Same bucket brigade, different thing standing still.

H. T. Kung and Charles Leiserson described these in 1978, long before there was anything worth running on one. The idea then sat around for thirty years waiting for a workload greedy enough to need it. Deep learning turned up.
